## Part 2: The Two-Stage Criteria System

The system uses two distinct sets of criteria for two different jobs:

**Stage 1 — SEARCH CRITERIA:** Used to identify and find candidates worth researching.
These are broad signals used to build a prospect list from databases,
LinkedIn, news, or referrals.

**Stage 2 — SCORING CRITERIA:** Used to assess a specific company after research.
These are weighted dimensions scored 0–10 each, totaling 0–100.

---

## Part 3: Stage 1 — Search / Identification Criteria

Use these criteria when generating a prospect list or evaluating whether
a company is worth researching at all. Any company meeting 3+ of these
signals is worth a qualification pass.

### Primary Search Signals (high-value indicators)

```
[ ] NYC-headquartered or has a major NYC office (500+ employees in metro)
[ ] Has a named CSR/ESG program, foundation, or dedicated giving page
[ ] Publicly funds food, nutrition, food justice, or hunger-related causes
[ ] Publicly funds K-12 education, youth development, or after-school programs
[ ] Has an employee volunteer program with paid VTO (Volunteer Time Off)
[ ] Has funded or partnered with a Harlem or upper Manhattan organization before
[ ] Is a 1% for the Planet member, B Corp, or has a public sustainability commitment
[ ] Has a board member, executive, or senior leader with known personal philanthropy
    in food, youth, or community development
[ ] Is in a sector with natural narrative fit:
    - Food & beverage / CPG
    - Healthcare / wellness / pharma
    - Financial services (community reinvestment angle)
    - Insurance (resilience / risk / community angle)
    - Retail (community presence, supply chain ethics)
    - Tech (STEM education, workforce development angle)
    - Real estate / construction (neighborhood transformation)
    - Media / entertainment (storytelling, community culture)
```

### Disqualifying Signals (deprioritize or skip)

```
[ ] No NYC presence — remote-first or HQ outside metro with no major office
[ ] Active ESG rollback or public CSR program elimination
[ ] Recent controversy around food safety, labor practices, or community harm
[ ] Already a confirmed Harlem Grown partner (flag as EXISTING — do not cold outreach)
[ ] Giving capacity clearly below $10K (too small to justify research investment)
[ ] Industry with no plausible narrative connection (mining, defense, etc.)
```

### Sector-Specific Search Queries

When building prospect lists programmatically, use these search angles:

```
"NYC CSR food access grant 2024"
"New York corporate employee volunteer day urban farm"
"1% for the Planet member New York"
"B Corp certified New York City"
"[Company] community investment Harlem"
"[Sector] ESG report food nutrition youth NYC"
"corporate sponsor urban agriculture New York"
"[Company] foundation youth education grant"
```

## Part 4: Stage 2 — Scoring Criteria (0–100 Total)

After research, score the company across 10 dimensions. Each is scored 0–10.
Threshold for outreach: **60/100 or higher** (previously 25/60 — revised upward
based on more granular criteria).

---

### Dimension 1: Mission Alignment — Food & Nutrition (0–10)
*Weight: HIGH. This is Harlem Grown's most distinctive asset.*

Does the company care about food access, food equity, nutrition, or healthy eating?

| Score | Evidence |
|---|---|
| 0 | No mention of food-related giving anywhere |
| 2 | Generic "healthy communities" language |
| 5 | Funds food banks or hunger relief generically |
| 7 | Explicitly funds food education or food justice |
| 9 | Named program or dedicated funding for food access/equity |
| 10 | Core business is food, or foundation's primary focus is food justice |

*Examples: Newman's Own = 10. Whole Foods = 9. Northwell Health = 7 (nutrition as part of wellness). FM Global = 4 (adjacent via "community resilience").*

---

### Dimension 2: Mission Alignment — Youth & Education (0–10)
*Weight: HIGH. HG's primary beneficiary is children ages 5–18.*

Does the company invest in K-12 education, youth development, or after-school programming?

| Score | Evidence |
|---|---|
| 0 | No youth focus |
| 3 | Occasional youth-related giving, not a priority |
| 5 | Youth is one of several CSR focus areas |
| 8 | Dedicated youth education or mentorship program |
| 10 | Youth is the primary CSR focus; named program, funded annually |

*Examples: Hasbro = 10 (children is core). Goldman Sachs = 7 (10,000 Small Businesses reaches youth adjacent). Northwell = 6.*

---

Does the company have environmental sustainability commitments that HG's
urban farming model can speak to?

| Score | Evidence |
|---|---|

| 5 | Sustainability report with community environment section |
| 7 | Active programs: urban greening, biodiversity, food systems |
| 10 | Sustainability is core identity; B Corp, 1% for Planet, or equivalent |

## Part 5: Scoring Summary & Thresholds

### Score Calculation

```
Total Score = Sum of all 10 dimensions (max 100)

Tier Assignment:
  PRIORITY (80–100)  → Immediate outreach. Strong match across the board.
                        Personalized letter + direct ask for site visit.

  STRONG   (60–79)   → Active pursuit. Good match with 1–2 gaps.
                        Personalized letter + tailored first ask.

  POSSIBLE (40–59)   → Worth a light-touch approach.
                        Template letter with personalization on 1–2 key points.
                        Monitor for ESG updates that could improve score.

  MONITOR  (25–39)   → Not ready now. Flag for re-evaluation in 6 months.
                        Something might change (new CSR direction, board shift).

  PASS     (<25)     → Skip. Don't invest research time.
```

### Score Weighting in Plain English

The dimensions most predictive of a successful HG partnership, based on the existing sponsor portfolio, in rough order of importance:

1. **Volunteer engagement appetite** — if they won't send people to the farm, the relationship won't deepen
2. **NYC proximity** — physical presence enables the hands-on experience that converts
3. **Food/nutrition mission alignment** — the most distinctive and emotionally resonant part of HG's pitch
4. **Youth/education mission alignment** — HG's primary beneficiary and most fundable program element
5. **Decision-maker accessibility** — the most frustrating gap when everything else is green

## Part 6: Company Archetype Profiles

These archetypes help the qualifier agent pattern-match quickly. When a company
fits an archetype, the agent knows which scoring dimensions to weight more heavily
and which program angles to surface in the letter.

---

### Archetype A: "The Mission Soulmate"
**Profile:** Food/beverage brand, natural products company, food justice foundation.
Their mission and HG's mission are nearly identical.
**Examples:** Newman's Own Foundation, Whole Foods Market, Hairstory
**Strongest scoring dimensions:** Food alignment (10), Values language match (9–10), Sustainability (8–9)
**Lead angle:** "You're already doing this work — we're the NYC chapter."
**First ask:** Co-brand a farm-to-table event. Product placement at Saturday programming.
**Expected score range:** 75–95

---

### Archetype B: "The Community Health Champion"
**Profile:** Hospital system, health insurance company, wellness brand, pharma with community programs.
They care about preventive health, nutrition education, and underserved communities.
**Examples:** Northwell Health, MetroPlusHealth, Mount Sinai
**Strongest scoring dimensions:** Youth/education (8), NYC proximity (9), Values language (7)
**Lead angle:** "We're practicing preventive medicine — 18,000 kids a year."
**First ask:** Wellness programming co-sponsorship. Mobile teaching kitchen partnership.
**Expected score range:** 65–85

---

### Archetype C: "The Resilient Financier"
**Profile:** Bank, insurance company, private equity firm with NYC community reinvestment mandate.
They have CRA (Community Reinvestment Act) obligations or established community investment programs.
**Examples:** FM Global, Wells Fargo, JPMorgan Chase, Goldman Sachs
**Strongest scoring dimensions:** Giving capacity (8–9), Decision-maker accessibility (via board), Longevity potential (8)
**Lead angle:** "Urban resilience starts with healthy communities and educated youth."
**First ask:** Employee volunteer day (30–50 staff on the farm). Most powerful demo for this archetype.
**Expected score range:** 60–80

---

### Archetype D: "The Purpose-Driven Consumer Brand"
**Profile:** DTC brand, retail company, or CPG with strong sustainability/community positioning.
They're in the 1% for the Planet orbit or B Corp ecosystem.
**Examples:** Patagonia Foundation, Eileen Fisher, Sweetgreen, Dig
**Strongest scoring dimensions:** Sustainability (9), Values language (8), Employee engagement (7)
**Lead angle:** "We're the urban farm version of what your brand stands for."
**First ask:** Cause marketing partnership. Pop-up event at one of their locations.
**Expected score range:** 55–75

---

### Archetype E: "The Tech Neighbor"
**Profile:** NYC-based tech company (or major office) with STEM education and workforce development CSR.
They care about the future talent pipeline and urban communities.
**Examples:** Google NYC, Salesforce, Etsy, Squarespace
**Strongest scoring dimensions:** Employee engagement (8), Youth/education (7), Giving capacity (8)
**Lead angle:** "STEM + sustainability. Your future workforce is growing up on these farms."
**First ask:** STEM learning day event — students tour the hydroponics greenhouse with tech framing.
**Expected score range:** 55–70

---

### Archetype F: "The Real Estate Transformer"
**Profile:** NYC real estate developer, property manager, or construction firm with community benefit commitments.
They care about neighborhood transformation and have Harlem-adjacent projects.
**Examples:** Related Companies, RXR Realty, L+M Development Partners
**Strongest scoring dimensions:** NYC/Harlem proximity (9–10), Neighborhood fit (9), Longevity potential (7)
**Lead angle:** "We transform abandoned lots too — just with seeds instead of steel."
**First ask:** Site visit + conversation about shared neighborhood investment strategy.
**Expected score range:** 50–70

## Part 7: Prospect Search Sources

When the system needs to generate a prospect list (not just evaluate a submitted company),
these are the sources to query:

### Structured Sources
- **IRS Form 990 databases** (ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer, Foundation Directory) — search for companies that have given to similar orgs (urban farms, food justice, NYC youth)
- **Candid / Foundation Directory Online** — corporate foundation giving history
- **NYC Council discretionary funding recipients** — shows which companies are investing in Harlem specifically
- **Crain's NYC "Corporate Citizenship" lists** — annual rankings of NYC corporate giving

### Signal Sources
- **LinkedIn** — search "CSR Director" or "VP Community Affairs" + "New York" + relevant sector
- **1% for the Planet member directory** — pre-qualified sustainability-aligned brands
- **B Corp directory** — filter by NYC, by revenue range
- **Points of Light Civic 50** — top community-minded companies, many NYC-headquartered
- **Benevity Grants database** — companies actively distributing grants to nonprofits

### Analogous Org Cross-Reference
These NYC nonprofits work with similar corporate sponsors — their annual reports and gala programs are a rich source of warm leads:
- Harlem Children's Zone (finance sector partners)
- City Harvest (food & beverage partners)
- GrowNYC (sustainability-aligned partners)
- Robin Hood Foundation (broad NYC corporate donor base)
- Rocking the Boat (youth + environment, South Bronx — different geography, overlapping sponsors)

## Part 8: Implementation in the Qualifier Agent

### How This Becomes Agent Instructions

The qualifier agent system prompt should encode:

1. **The 10 scoring dimensions** with exact rubrics (copy from Part 4)
2. **The archetype list** (Part 6) so the agent can pattern-match and surface the right angle
3. **The disqualifying signals** (Part 3) so it can fast-fail obvious misses
4. **The threshold table** (Part 5) so it outputs the correct tier, not just a score

### Structured Output the Qualifier Should Return

```python
class QualificationResult(BaseModel):
    company_name: str
    website_url: str

    # 10 dimension scores
    scores: dict = {
        "food_nutrition_alignment": int,          # 0-10
        "youth_education_alignment": int,         # 0-10
        "environmental_sustainability": int,       # 0-10
        "nyc_harlem_proximity": int,              # 0-10
        "employee_volunteer_appetite": int,        # 0-10
        "giving_capacity": int,                   # 0-10
        "esg_values_language_match": int,         # 0-10
        "decision_maker_accessibility": int,       # 0-10
        "sector_narrative_fit": int,              # 0-10
        "partnership_longevity_potential": int,    # 0-10
    }

    total_score: int                  # sum of above, 0-100
    tier: str                         # PRIORITY | STRONG | POSSIBLE | MONITOR | PASS
    archetype: str                    # A-F from Part 6, or "None"
    go_no_go: bool                    # True if total >= 60

    key_signals: list[str]            # 3-5 verbatim evidence quotes from their site
    strongest_angle: str              # The single most compelling pitch hook
    biggest_gap: str                  # The weakest dimension and why
    recommended_program_match: str    # Which HG program to lead with
    decision_maker_hypothesis: str    # Who probably owns this decision and why
    existing_partner_flag: bool       # True if they're already an HG partner
```

### Fast-Fail Logic

Before running the full 10-dimension score, the qualifier should check:
1. Is this company already in Harlem Grown's known partner list? → Flag + stop
2. Does the company have a NYC presence at all? → If not, score NYC dimension 0 and
   note this will likely push them below threshold
3. Is there a CSR/giving page at all? → If not, score capacity and accessibility low immediately

## Part 9: Updating These Criteria

This framework should be treated as a living document. Update it when:

- Harlem Grown adds a new partner (add to the sponsor profile table, validate against scoring)
- A PRIORITY-scored prospect declines — analyze the gap between score and outcome
- A low-scored company becomes a partner — find the signal we missed
- HG's programs shift (new offerings create new matching angles)

The goal is to have the scoring get more accurate over time by grounding it
in actual outcomes, not just theoretical alignment.
