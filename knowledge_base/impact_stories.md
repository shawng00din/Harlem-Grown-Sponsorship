## Story: Marcus and the First Tomato
**Tags:** #youth-transformation #food-access #hands-on-learning
**Best for:** Food companies, healthcare companies, any archetype

Marcus was 9 when he joined Harlem Grown's school program at PS 175. His teacher
described him as disengaged — the kid who stared out the window. The first week,
Marcus was assigned to tend a tomato bed. He watered it every morning before class.
Eight weeks later, he picked his first ripe tomato, cut it open himself, and tasted it.
"I grew this," he told his teacher. It was the first vegetable he'd ever eaten.
By spring, Marcus was running the school garden tour for visiting parents. His teacher
says he's the most engaged student in the class.

## Story: A Goldman Sachs Volunteer Day
**Tags:** #employee-engagement #community #volunteer
**Best for:** Financial services, insurance (Archetype C) — shows ROI of volunteer day

Forty Goldman Sachs employees arrived at the 128th Street farm on a Thursday in October.
By noon, they had cleared a new planting bed, installed raised beds, and harvested
three hundred pounds of kale and collards. A senior VP who'd never touched soil before
said it was the most grounding thing she'd done in years. Two weeks later, Goldman
returned — this time bringing ten employees' children for a Saturday tour.
Their employee satisfaction scores for the day: 4.9/5.

## Story: The Family Table
**Tags:** #food-access #family-impact #community
**Best for:** Food companies, healthcare (Archetypes A, B)

Rosa, a grandmother raising three grandchildren in the Frederick Douglass Houses,
started coming to HG's weekly produce stand on 140th Street in 2019. She'd never
been able to afford fresh vegetables regularly. Now she comes every week. "My
grandchildren ask what we're having for dinner. Before, they didn't care. Now
Destiny wants to know if we have the kale." Her oldest granddaughter, Destiny, 14,
joined the Saturday Leadership Program the following spring.

## Story: From Farm to First Job
**Tags:** #workforce #youth-transformation #economic-mobility
**Best for:** Financial services, tech, real estate (Archetypes C, E, F)

Jerome came to HG's Workforce Development program at 20, after aging out of foster
care with no employment history. He had no resume, no references, no idea how to
work in an office. He spent six months learning soil science, composting systems,
and farm operations. HG connected him to a paid position at a GrowNYC community
garden. Today, at 23, he manages two community garden sites and is completing his
Associate's degree in environmental science. "HG didn't just teach me how to grow
things. They taught me I could grow."

## Story: The Hydroponics Greenhouse
**Tags:** #sustainability #environment #STEM #youth-transformation
**Best for:** Tech companies, sustainability brands (Archetypes D, E)

Harlem Grown's hydroponic greenhouse on 132nd Street grows lettuce, herbs, and
microgreens year-round — no soil, no seasons, just water and light. Every Saturday
morning, a rotating group of students runs the system: checking pH levels, monitoring
pump pressure, harvesting. Thirteen-year-old Aaliyah, who wants to be an engineer,
says the greenhouse is "basically a computer made of plants." She's right. The
greenhouse has become the entry point for students interested in STEM — a gateway
from a grow light to a career in science.

## Story: What the Gala Dinner Started
**Tags:** #leadership #community #partnership-longevity
**Best for:** Showing depth of partnership — useful for Archetype C financiers, board-connected prospects

FM Global's board member Angie Henderson Moncada first heard about Harlem Grown at
a gala five years ago. She signed up for a volunteer day, brought her daughter.
Her daughter — 11 at the time — helped plant a raised bed. Her daughter is now 16
and has volunteered at the farm six more times. Angie sits on HG's board. FM Global's
employees have logged over 400 volunteer hours at Harlem Grown farms. This is what
it looks like when a corporate partnership deepens over time: it becomes personal.

## Story: The Neighborhood That Grew Together
**Tags:** #community #neighborhood-transformation #environment
**Best for:** Real estate companies, community-focused brands (Archetype F, D)

Three years ago, the lot on 140th Street was an illegal dumping site — broken glass,
mattresses, discarded appliances. Today it's a working urban farm with eight raised
beds, a composting station, and a rain water collection system. The neighbors who
walked past it now stop to pick up their weekly produce box. The teenagers who tagged
the fence are some of HG's most reliable Saturday volunteers. Harlem Grown didn't
just build a farm. They transformed a piece of the neighborhood.
